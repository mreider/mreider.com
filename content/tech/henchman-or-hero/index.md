I recently spoke with the CEO of a small, profitable startup. Let's call him Mike. His company pulls in a few million dollars a year with a lean team. They've made it past the survival stage, but now they're grappling with the next big question: how to grow.

Mike's team is stuck on a familiar startup dilemma: which user to prioritize. Mike wants to focus on buyers, adding features like loyalty programs, promotions, and customer acquisition tools. His team is more focused on the practitioners - reducing pain points, automating workflows, and fixing bugs. This is a split in who the team believes matters most.

Startups rarely have the resources to chase both paths at once. Mike already had a preference but hesitated to articulate it. When he asked me whether a product management course, or a PM hire, might help his team with prioritization, I wasn't sure if he was looking for clarity or validation.

---

## Vision vs. Execution

This isn't unique. It's common for founders and teams to have trouble agreeing on what's important. Early on, founders are the product managers. But as their responsibilities grow, they spend less time in the trenches and are surprised when their team is working on things they never agreed to.

This is the case for Mike. He now spends most of his time pitching to buyers and closing deals, while his team fights fires back at the office. Their priorities diverge. Mike can't micromanage every decision, but the company has drifted from its original direction.

---

## Reflecting on My Own Experience

This reminds me of my time as a PM at the Y-Combinator startup, Checkr, where I was the second PM, working under a talented but relatively inexperienced PM director. One of our biggest jobs was translating the founder's vision into an actionable roadmap. We had our own thoughts and ideas, and eventually we'd influence things directly, but at the beginning our jobs were about sailing towards the founder's compass point without challenging much.

This phase was not about big-picture strategy. It was simple alignment. Our jobs weren't about what to build; we were there to keep the team connected to the bigger picture, especially when the work felt reactive or tedious.

---

## What Mike Needs in a First PM

When Mike and I talked a few weeks later, he said he was thinking about hiring a product manager instead of enrolling his team in a strategy course. Probably the right move. But it got me thinking about what kind of PM a company like Mike's needs.

Broadly speaking, PMs fall into two camps: execution-focused and strategy-focused.

- **Execution PMs** are great at taking high-level priorities and turning them into actionable plans. The risk is that they can feel like project managers, moving tickets in Jira without influencing the product's direction.

- **Strategy PMs** focus on the big picture: market trends, customer needs, and long-term vision. They're necessary for growth but less useful when a team is struggling with day-to-day decisions.

For a company at Mike's stage, the immediate need is for an execution-focused PM. Someone to bridge the gap between Mike's vision and the team's daily work. Influencing the strategy matters eventually, but right now, the team needs help remembering the right trade-off decisions.
